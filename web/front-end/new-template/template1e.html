Big data

We're building a predictive model based on the most successful efforts of online retail giants - here's how we're doing it!

Here's the problem

Every business would love to be able to predict the future. In the absence of affordable time travel or a reliable psychic, many companies rely on data analytics to look ahead. Our team set out to create a predictive model of purchases given available customer data. If we have many points of interest about what a potential buyer is looking for, how do we know what information is important? Is it possible to build an application that can, in a sense, read the customer's mind?

We're a small group of graduate students at the University of Colorado attempting to answer these questions.

Here's our solution

A 2012 Hackathon project at Kaggle invited teams to create a project to predict purchases based on queries. The data set is a record of mobile queries made on Best Buy's website over a period of two years. The challenge is to make that data work to offer the best possible suggestion for any search made on that website. Teams are asked to create an algorithm that produces at least five suggested products for anything any user enters. Essentially, the project aims to recreate the groundbreaking predictive features made famous by Amazon and Google.

Our solution approaches the problem using machine learning algorithms and emerging analytic technologies. We have chosen to store our data set on a Hadoop, a nonrelational database that is enjoying increasing popularity in the start-up world. Our machine learning model is based on application of Bayes' theorem in statistics. In the algorithm we used, all features of a data are assumed to be independent from one another. This algorithm is commonly known as naive Bayes.

About our team

Our team met to collaborate on a group project for the Big Data course in Spring 2014. Together, we have experience as data scientists, computer engineers, applied mathematicians, physicists and software developers. Our team consists of Lei Bao, Alexandra Martin, Seyedeh Mortazavi and Gregory Petropoulos.
